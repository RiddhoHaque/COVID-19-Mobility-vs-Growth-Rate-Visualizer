How Different Countries reacted to COVID19: A Data Centric Analysis

Around the world, the pandemic caused by the novel coronavirus has caused governments to enforce strict lockdown protocols in a bid to decelerate the spread of the virus and reduce the mounting pressure on healthcare systems by promoting social distancing. This has caused a large number of educational institutions, businesses and offices to either shut down completely or carry out their operations remotely when possible. Thus, more and more people around the world are now spending large amounts of time at home. For many, the everyday commute to work has been non-existent for a while.

Google’s COVID-19 Community Mobility Reports show how communities worldwide are now experiencing a lot less human movement than what they used to. This dataset documents how visits and duration of stays in groceries and pharmacies, retail and recreation spots, parks, public transportation hubs, workplaces and residential areas in different countries has changed starting from February 15, 2020. The information behind this is collected by aggregating the location data of users who have turned on the location history setting in their devices. The change in mobility is expressed as the percentage deviation from pre-defined baseline values, which represent the usual levels of community mobility before COVID-19 spread across the world. Separate baseline values were calculated for each country for each day of the week by taking the median values of the community’s mobility from January 3 - February 6, 2020. For instance, the reports show that on Sunday, 5th April, 2020, the deviation in mobility in workplaces in Bangladesh was -54%, meaning that the number of visits and duration of stays in workplaces on that date was 54% less than what was normally seen on Sundays during the time period of Jan 03- Feb 06.

While similar community mobility trends have been published by Apple, Facebook and other sources, Google’s reports document the change in visits to different types of places separately. In other words, google’s reports allow us to discern reductions in visits to workplaces, malls, public transport stations and recreation spots from mobility changes in residential areas, grocery stores and pharmacies.

Here, we combine Google’s community mobility data with the COVID19 related data provided by John Hopkins University CSSE. JHU CSSE’s data provides a time series of the total number of confirmed COVID-19 cases, deaths and recovered patients in each country worldwide. Combining the two datasets, we see how and when COVID19 spread in Italy, Bangladesh, Vietnam, Sweden, New Zealand, Singapore, Indonesia, Spain, Portugal, UK, Japan and Sri Lanka, when and to what extent these countries immobilized their communities, and how immobilization helped to “flatten the curve” in these nations. Finally, we visualize the effect community immobilization has on stemming the growth rate of COVID19 cases.

Fig 1. A bubble chart animation showing how community mobilization in different countries varied in response to the COVID19 pandemic. Bubble sizes are representative of population densities, the bigger the bubble, the higher the density.

The infographic in Figure 1 visualizes how the number of active cases and the reduction in community mobility occurred concurrently over time from February 15 to June 27 in the aforementioned countries. By active cases, we refer to the number of alive patients in those countries who have not completely recovered. This is determined by subtracting the number of fatalities and recovered patients from the number of total confirmed cases in each day. In Figure 1, each country is represented by a bubble whose size differs according to the country’s population density. Since COVID-19 is propagated largely by human contact, countries with higher densities (i. e. larger bubbles) may have to adopt largely different policies in order to keep the growth in the number of cases in check. The infographic shows a time series representing the state of each country in terms of mobility and number of active cases, with the corresponding date shown in the chart title written above. The X-axis, representing the number of active cases, is plotted using a logarithmic scale. For this and all visualizations that follow, we shall use the logarithmic scale while plotting the number of active cases, in order to make useful visualizations which model the exponential growth in the number of COVID-19 cases. In terms of the Y-axis, which measures the change in community mobility, we take the three day running average of mobility deviations in workplaces, public transport hubs and retail and recreation spots.

On February 15, 2020, the first date since which community mobility data has been made public by google, Japan, Singapore, UK and Vietnam already had 43, 72, 18 and 16 confirmed cases respectively, while the number of confirmed cases in the rest of the countries included in our analysis remained below 10.

However, Figure 1 demonstrates that the first true “explosion” in the number of cases among these countries happened in Italy. By 10th March 2020, the number of confirmed cases in Italy had crossed 10,000, prompting their government to impose stringent lockdown measures and reducing community mobility to less than 60% of the baseline. Spain and Portugal soon followed the same course of action. Both these countries maintained high levels of community immobilization till the number of active cases started decreasing, which is when their mobility levels slowly began to rise again. This is shown in Figures 2-4, which map the trajectory of Italy, Spain and Portugal in the bubble chart of Figure 1 respectively.

The examples so far indicate that mass lockdowns have proven to be effective in controlling the number of COVID19 infections in many countries. However, as seen in the case of Bangladesh, it is as important for lockdown protocols to sustain for a substantial amount of time as it is for lockdowns to be initiated early on. Sadly, sustained lockdowns may often not be feasible in many cases due to a number of socio-economic reasons.

In this regard, Singapore’s early approach towards controlling the spread of COVID-19 provides an interesting alternative. Despite COVID-19 patients being identified in Singapore before the virus even had its name, Singapore managed to control the spread of the virus quite effectively, by ensuring extensive testing of suspected cases, employing extensive contract tracing programmes and health checks at their airports. Thus, despite its significantly high population density and the absence of country-wide lockdowns, Singapore enjoyed early success in controlling the virus. The lockdown did help flatten the curve, and towards late June, the citizens of Singapore had slowly begun to mobilize once again. Japan, on the other hand, offers a different type of success story. As the trajectory of Japan in Figure 1, shown in Figure 19, reveals, Japan avoided declaring mass lockdowns, and decrease in community mobility is certainly not as low as we saw earlier in countries like Italy, Spain and New Zealand. Even the little downward spike revealed in Figure 19 lasted a very short time, signifying that Japan avoided prolonged lockdowns throughout. However, the number of active cases in a single day in Japan never crossed the 10,000 mark [as of June 27, 2020], which can be seen as significant success considering Japan was one of first few countries to which COVID19 spread.

Fig 19: The trajectory of Japan in Figure 1

Further analysis in Figure 20 also shows that the peak in terms of active cases in Japan has passed, and also shows that the reduction in mobility in Japan lasted a very short time as well, and that was only when the number of active cases had peaked. In all, Japan has so far managed to cope up extremely well in the face of the global pandemic, despite mass lockdown initiative. Reports accredit this success to Japan’s cultural values, which promotes wearing masks in public, avoiding handshakes and the general compliance of the Japanese people to government instructions. Japanese Deputy Prime Minister Taro Aso has been quoted as saying,  'Between your country and our country, mindo (the level of people) is different.' While alternate explanations may exist as well, Japan certainly proves to be an interesting case when it comes down to reacting to COVID-19.

Fig 20. Timeline of Active Cases in Japan

While the Japanese relied on subtle but effective measures against Coronavirus, Vietnam on the other hands took authoritative measures early on to produce an effective response, even without lockdown measures. Figure 21, which documents the trajectory of Vietnam in the bubble chart, shows that despite not imposing prolonged mass lockdowns, the number of active cases in Vietnam has not even reached the 1000 mark so far. In fact Vietnam never had more than 25 new cases confirmed in a single day. So far, the total number of confirmed cases in Vietnam is 370 with 0 fatalities.

Fig 21. Trajectory of Vietnam

Even when we consider their active cases curve, Vietnam’s success story remains intact.  Figure 22 shows that a reduction of community mobility below the 40% mark was seen for about half a month, meaning that mass immobilization has not been required so far.

Fig 22. Timeline of Active Cases in Vietnam

Reports accredit Vietnam’s success to gatekeeping, closing its borders to China the moment COVID19 surfaced, keeping schools closed, and quarantining all incoming passengers who came from a country having at least one confirmed COVID-19 cases immediately after they arrived. All quarantined passengers were tested, regardless of whether they showed symptoms or not. Passengers were quarantined in quarantine centres, in contrast to the home quarantine approach followed in many other countries. The costs for quarantining passengers was borne by the government. This large scale quarantining seems to have benefitted Vietnam, with 0 COVID-19 related fatalities to account for thus far. 

In summary, our analysis of the countries reveals that while early initiation of lockdown and sustaining mass immobility seems to have benefitted many countries, doing so may often be infeasible due to a plethora of factors. Japan, Singapore and Vietnam have all done well without resorting to prolonged mass immobilization. However, these countries had alternative measures in place, such as closing borders, health checkups at the borders, enforcing mass quarantine to all incoming passengers coming from COVID-19 affected countries. Countries which did not have alternative measures and who did not resort to mass immobilization for prolonged periods, like Sweden and Indonesia seem to be still dealing with an increasing number of COVID-19 patients.

Thus, the higher the doubling rate, the faster is the virus’s spread. To check if a reduction in mobility truly reduces the doubling rate of the virus, we take the data of all countries which are listed in Google’s mobility reports. For each country, for each day T, we calculate the average reduction in mobility to workplaces, public transport hubs, recreation and retail spots for the days in the interval [T, T+13] and check the corresponding doubling rate in the number of active cases in the days [T+14, T+27] given that the required data is available. The two week gap between the mobility and the doubling rate is kept to include the effect of the incubation period of COVID-19, which may be as high as 14 days, according to WHO. We only plot points whose number of active patients in the day T+14 is above 1000, and whose number of active patients in day T+27 is greater than the number of active patients in day T+14. This is done to see the effect of community immobilization in instances in which the curve of active cases is rising and the number of cases indicate that community transmission is in effect.

This leaves us with 2418 data points and results in the downward curve of Figure 23. This, along with the correlation coefficient of -0.48 indicates that mass immobility might be of great help when community transmission is present and the number of patients is on the rise.

In all, the novel coronavirus keeps presenting new challenges to governments around the world. While different countries react to it differently, the long term effects of such policies remains to be seen. However, our analysis does indicate that community immobilization helps slow down the spread, and that sustained lockdown may indeed help keep the number of cases in check. Whether that is feasible, however, is a much more complex question and answers to questions like what can or should be done may vary from country to country.
